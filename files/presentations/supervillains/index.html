Gravitational waves

Supervillains all across the world watch the physics and astronomy news very closely. Whenever a new physical phenomenon is discovered, we immediately begin scheming about how we can best turn it to our advantage. So I hope you all paid close attention to the announcement last year of the first detection of gravitational waves. These ripples in the fabric of spacetime are produced strongly when two massive objects like black holes or neutron stars meet. In particular, we supervillains have spent a year discussing the implications of one particular video.

Gravitational waves

Perhaps you have seen this before, but if not, here is what's happening. Gravitational waves are washing over Earth. As they do so, they squash and stretch it slightly. Now, I'm sure I don't need to tell you how powerful this idea is! Imagine if we could beam gravitational waves at a planet until it literally tears itself apart!

Class! What is the problem with this picture? The eagle-eyed among you will have spotted this in the corner. [Scale of Effect Vastly Exaggerated] The problem is that gravity is very weak. It's the weakest of the fundamental four forces.

How close?

The measured signal came from a distance of 1 billion light years away, far far outside our galaxy. And the effect of the wave when it hit us was tiny. The Earth stretched by one thousandth of the width of a proton over the 4km length of the detector. So how close would these merging black holes have to be in order to generate gravitational waves big enough to rip the Earth apart? Within our galaxy?

How close?

Within our solar system, even? Well, yes. Our top Supervillain Research scientists have been running the numbers. Here is how close you would have to be to really feel those gravitational waves...

It's about 100 km. You would need a pair of black holes in London. Of course, the real danger here isn't gravitational waves any more. It's spaghettification: the immense pull from being so close to a black hole would stretch the Earth out like a strand of spaghetti. Therefore, in order to destroy the Earth, you could simply place it close to a black hole, or make one yourself. They're quite small. Very portable, really.

This plan ticks all the supervillain check boxes. It is impressive. Imagine the Earth disappearing into a black hole, or being torn apart by a pair of them! It is ingenious: no-one will EVER suspect that you are trying to create a black hole right in London. And it is completely impractical. In order to achieve this, you will need to collect enough matter from 60 suns and compress it into a space smaller than the M25. THIS is truly a plan worthy of a supervillain's career!

Some of you might be wondering why we don't go about this another way. Why don't we simply take the Sun and compress it until it becomes a black hole? All you would need to do is crush it until it was about the same size as Cambridge. But this would achieve nothing! The Sun would still have exactly the same mass as before and so the Earth would happily to continue to orbit around it! No, the only way to utterly destroy the planet with a black hole would be to put them very, very close to each other.

The inverse square law

Students, take careful notes! This is an excellent example of something that is a constant thorn in the side of many unsuspecting supervillains: the inverse-square-law. In general, energy sources fall off with the square of the distance from the source. For example, if we view a lightbulb from twice as far, the energy from it is spread over an area four times as large. This is true not only for light. It is why the gravity of the Sun cannot rip us apart.

By the way, this is why so many of the best evil plans involve lasers. By focusing energy into a beam, we can avoid losing it to all the directions where it's not needed! But even a laser spreads out with distance and so your lasers will need to be VERY narrow if you are going to fire them a long way. If you don't put enough energy in then you will be a laughing stock!

Yes, this is an absolutely classic case of a villain who didn't do his calculations right. I expect you to all do better. And this leads us on very nicely to our next topic, which is...

Perhaps you wish to fry the Earth. This is a noble goal indeed. But, as we have just seen, your biggest enemies here are energy and the inverse-square-law.

Focused Energy

By now you will understand that the answer to this is to focus energy in some way. Before we proceed, I must strongly advise against the use of a spaceship-mounted laser weapon. Although it may seem like a good idea at the time, your spaceship will inevitably become the target for every rebel alliance in the galaxy. No, to succeed in frying the Earth, you need to be a little more clever.

Your first option is to harness the power of raw sunlight: build mirrors and lots of them. A difficult problem will be how to keep these mirrors pointed at the Earth as it moves around the Sun. Thankfully, there is an astrophysical phenomenon that will help you with this.

Lagrange Points

The Lagrange points, or "nature's parking spots", where gravitational and orbital forces cancel out and a satellite can remain stationary without having to burn fuel. Many satellites launched now are positioned at Lagrange points, in particular L2. The fools! Little do they know that your array of super-reflectors will soon be joining them! Of course, that's not your only problem. You will need a trillion square miles of mirrors to focus enough energy on the Earth to melt it. But what if there an easier way? Thankfully, astrophysics already produces focused jets of energy for us.

Gamma-ray bursts

Gamma-ray bursts are believed to be produced by the collapse of enormous stars into black holes or neutron stars, or by neutron stars merging. They are the brightest things in the universe, and their energy is highly focused into beams that span unimaginable distances. This would seem to be the ideal force for a supervillain to harness! But they fall down in two respects.

First, we have no control over them. They originate from far outside our galaxy, at distances we can only dream of reaching within our lifetimes. And here the inverse-square law strikes again: even these are the brightest things in the universe, they are weak and feeble by the time they reach us. We have no way to cause a gamma-ray burst within the Milky Way where it might be able to do damage. But more importantly, even the almighty power of a gamma ray burst would leave the Earth's physical form virtually untouched! It would be deadly to life, but an ambitious supervillain with the primary goal of reducing our planet to rubble would be sadly disappointed.

Bring the sun to us

Perhaps there is another way. Perhaps we could bring the sun to us instead? But how would we do this? How would you move the Sun?

[...]

We can't attach rockets to it. But what if we put a hat on the sun? Imagine making an enormous mirror that reflects half of the light from the sun. Because light carries a tiny bit of energy and momentum with it, reflecting the light like this would cause the Sun to start moving on the direction of the mirror. If we very carefully balance the radiation pressure on the mirror and the gravity from the Sun, the mirror will stay in the same place over the Sun while the Sun speeds up and up.

This is called a Shkadov Engine, after Dr. Leonid Mikhailovich Shkadov. Dr. Shkadov, we salute you for your work: only a supervillain of the highest order could come up with such an idea!

The problem with this is that it is a veeeeery slow way to move anything. Any guesses as to how fast our sun would be moving after, say a million years? ...It's about 40 miles per hour. After one million years our sun wouldn't even be breaking the motorway speed limit! And what's worse, the gravity of the Sun would just drag the Earth along with it anyway! No, this is a terrible idea for destroying the Earth.
